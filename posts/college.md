I will be graduating this year with a Bachelors of Science in Computer Science. Looking back at the last four years of my degree however, I think its pretty fraudulent.

> TL-DR; My computer science degree is actually a very confused software engineering degree that was told its a computer science degree its entire life.
> 
> A queer degree perhaps?

# Computer Science is a Real Degree Though?

I'm graduating from the University of Kansas and my degree has been ABET accredited[^acredidation]. But that's not what I mean by "fake" degree. What I actually mean is that everything I've learned though four years of education doesn't actually amount to any **real** knowledge of computer science. Everything that I've been taught has only given me a fragmented and incomplete view of what **computer science** actually is.

# Computer Science... or Software Engineering?

[Computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science) has been and still is a study based in **math**. It predates the actual invention of computing machinery and its practical applications by decades. Computer scientists were solving problems and creating algorithms years and years before they could be practically implemented. The reason they were able to do any of that is because they had a solid foundation in math. 

Yet the classes that I took skipped all the important math that would be extremely useful in understanding computer science. In fact everything relevant was just crammed into or two classes, `Theory of Computing` and `Discrete Structures`. Discrete structures was particularly egregious as it skimmed over huge fields of math such as abstract algebra, linear algebra, and statistics. Instead I took classes like `Embedded Systems`, `Operating Systems`, and `Software Engineering I-II`.

To me this was a glaring indication that I wasn't being taught computer science, but I was being taught **software engineering**.

# Software Engineering is Fine... But

To be clear, I don't have any issue with learning Software Engineering. Its not a bad degree to have and you learn a fair bit about computer science along the way. I just think that computer science and software engineering are not one and the same.

Software Engineering requires that people work together to build and maintain a software product. This requires more than just technical knowledge or "computer science". Computer science on the other hand is more so an academic pursuit and deeply math based. 

To me, advertising a degree as "computer science" but actually teaching "software engineering" seems like false advertising at best and fraud at worst.

# Okay So You Actually Got a Software Engineering Degree Instead. So What?

Well, I think the quality of my degree suffered because of this "false advertisement". I didn't actually get a complete "computer science" degree (which was given to me by the School of Engineering by the way) and because of that my "software engineering" degree just fell short of my expectations.

Nobody *properly* taught us how to use git, GitHub to collaborate with multiple people or view and make code changes. It was a measly one week mention and a small lab which just made us upload code to GitHub. I was never taught how to use the terminal effectively. Tools like tmux, and shell scripting weren't even mentioned (as far as I remember). Instead I got a strange mix of half baked theory and only slightly useful practical knowledge.

There were only a handful of good classes which, mostly thanks to the professors, taught me a lot about either Computer Science or Software Engineering.

# The Impact

Most of my growth and learning came from personal interest and practical knowledge gained from internships and projects I took on. This is a huge problem for the people graduating with a CS degree. Being able to graduate with fragmented theoretical and practical knowledge means the learning curve right out of college is insanely higher, and graduates don't actually know what to expect. Among my classmates its almost painfully obvious who has just taken classes without taking any extra initiative and who has put in extra time or effort to learn more. It reflects poorly on the value of my degree when people can graduate with practically no zero knowledge of what they can do or should be able to do.

While I'd agree that doing more than the bare minimum is to be expected if you're pursuing a college degree I don't think that's the issue with a CS degree. The **real** issue is that my degree is so fragmented across the entire field. I understand that you need a fair understanding of computer science to get a software engineering degree, but in this day and age (especially with AI on the rise) I feel like its becoming less and less relevant. Computer science as a study is not dead however, we still need people to reason about computing in a mathematical manner, and in fact lots of cutting edge math-based research in formal verification (for software and AI) and type systems is being done right now.

# A Change to My Degree(s)?

I propose that "Computer Science" as a degree should be put together as a math-heavy degree which really just extends or modifies the traditional Math degree with more classes geared to toward the practical applications of Math in computing. In fact I'm taking a great class right now, **Math 601: Cryptography**, which teaches us the math behind crypto systems and how they're actually applied to computing. Classes such as these and other ones that I took like Static Analysis, or Programming Languages II (which examined the mathematical structures behind functional programming languages) are much more connected to the domain of computer science.

"Software Engineering" on the other hand should be its own degree, very similar to the "Computer Science" degree that I got, but with more emphasis on collaboration and use of developer tools (yes including AI). Especially with the way software engineering is heading these days its more important than ever that graduates know how to build and develop with tools, read and write code effectively, and communicate well with others.
