# Background

As you may have inferred, or likely already known, I graduated with a Bachelors of Science in Computer Science from the School of Engineering at The University of Kansas this past year.

I'd say the last four years of college were a pretty great life experience. I had lots of fun hanging out with friends and being hard at work while hardly working. Looking back at the last four years of my education, however, I have... mixed feelings to say the least. 

Even after I finished my first two semesters I'd always felt like I was missing something, or knew that something was missed. The curriculum *felt* incomplete and unpolished, put together in a haphazard sort of manner really. This could probably be said about quite a few degree programs at many universities, but I'm talking about Computer Science in particular because I strongly feel that it is entirely misrepresented and honestly not at all what it should be.

# Computer Science is a Real Degree Though?

Yea I know, the title is a hook lol.

What I actually mean is that everything I've learned though four years of education hasn't given me a real look into computer science. Everything that I've been taught has only given me a fragmented and incomplete view of what **computer science** actually is.

# Computer Science is Math

[Computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science) has been and still is a study based in **mathematics**. It predates the actual invention of computing machinery and its practical applications by decades. Computer scientists were solving problems and creating algorithms years and years before they could be practically implemented. The reason they were able to do any of that is because they had a solid foundation in math.

When I refer to "math" as I did above I'm not just referring to the *three calculus courses* I was made to take as a part of my degree. While they have their own purpose, other fields of math such as **discrete math**, **linear algebra**, **number theory**, and **probability and statistics** are also very applicable to the field of computer science. Cutting edge research in type theory also uses a very new, and frankly insane in my opinion, field of math called [Category Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory) which I would describe as an advanced form of [Abstract Algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra).

Yet the classes that I took merely skimmed all the important math that would be extremely useful in understanding computer science. In fact, everything I found relevant was crammed into just about four classes, `Intro to Linear Algebra`, `Probability and Statistics`, `Theory of Computing`, `Discrete Structures`. Discrete structures was particularly egregious as it tried to cram multiple fields of math into one class. It felt like we were learning something entirely different every week.

# Computer Science... or Software Engineering?

Instead I took classes like Embedded Systems, Operating Systems, and... **Software Engineering I-II**.

As one of my favorite math professors at KU would say:

> *Whoa.* - Jeff Lang

It's almost like I was learning how to be a software engineer, and not a computer scientist.

# Software Engineering is Fine... But

To be clear, I don't have any issue with learning Software Engineering. It's not a bad degree to have and you need to learn a fair bit about computer science along the way. I just think that **computer science and software engineering are not one and the same**.

Although likely not the intention of my University, it seems disingenuous to promise that you will teach students computer science, and instead teach them software engineering.

# Okay So You Actually Got a Software Engineering Degree Instead. So What?

Well, I think the quality of my degree suffered because of this "false advertisement". I didn't actually get a complete "computer science" degree like my degree says (which was given to me by the School of Engineering by the way) and because of that my "software engineering" degree just fell short of my expectations.

Nobody *properly* taught us how to use git, and GitHub to collaborate with multiple people or view and make code changes. It was a measly one week mention and a small lab which just made us upload code to GitHub. I was never taught how to use the terminal effectively which is more than likely an essential part of a software engineer's job. Tools like `tmux`, and shell scripting weren't even mentioned (as far as I remember). Instead I got a strange mix of half baked theory and only slightly useful practical knowledge.

Additionally, Software Engineering requires that people work together to build and maintain a software product. This requires more than just technical knowledge or "computer science". The classes we took gave us some of the practical knowledge we needed to be successful, but never actually *taught* us to work in a team. The only exception were the two Software Engineering classes which were unfortunately taught by a *particularly incompetent* professor. While I will not name him, if you ever encounter a KU EECS student or alumni and ask them about the worst professor in the department his name will likely come up.

# The Marshmallows in My Lucky Charms

While I haven't been painting my degree in the best light there were a handful of great classes which, in part thanks to the professors, taught me a lot about either Computer Science or Software Engineering. For any KU Students, or those interested I'll highlight a few of the particularly good classes I took below:

**Programming Languages I** was a set of classes that focused on functional language interpreters. Specifically the structure of functional languages, and how they are evaluated. We started with a small subset of a language (just arithmetic operations) and slowly built an entire working language which included variables, functions and even recursion and basic data structures. It was truly an eye opening class that actually helped me understand how [functional programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_programming) worked and how features in other languages borrowed from this style of programming. The professor who taught this class for me, [Dr. Perry Alexander](https://perry.alexander.name/), is also excellent and had many fun and highly interesting and informational anecdotes and tangents he would go on which I greatly enjoyed. He is, in my opinion, one of the best professors in the department and you should not miss the chance to take a class with him.

**Programming Languages II** is technically designated as a follow up to the previous class but is not entirely related. This class focused on the mathematical proofs which verified that a functional language was complete and correct. We learned [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus) (a language consisting of only functions) and even implemented boolean and arithmetic operators which was very cool. NOTE for KU students - the class was also taught by Dr. Perry Alexander but he mentioned that the class and its structure was course content was subject to change soon.

**Software Reverse Engineering** was a class that has us use tools to disassemble and make sense of binaries. It was very hands on and the professor, [Prasad Kulkarni](https://eecs.ku.edu/people/prasad-kulkarni), gave us plenty of binaries to disassemble and make sense of. In fact most of the homework and even the midterm and final was consisted of examining binaries and using different techniques to extract a string or flag. It really helped solidify how assembly works and how compilers translate high-level constructs into low-level code. It also gave me a really cool set of tools to help me untangle any future binaries.

**Compiler Construction** is also another great class that I already have an entire blog post on, you can read it [here](/post/learn-to-build-a-compiler).

All of those classes were electives that I chose to take but I strongly believe at least Programming Languages I and Compiler Construction **should** be a part of the required coursework. Those classes in particular helped me understand computer science and software engineering practically and theoretically.

# The Impact

Most of my growth and learning about software engineering and computer science came from personal interest and practical knowledge gained from internships and projects, along with a few *optional* classes I took. This is a huge problem for the people graduating with a CS degree. Being able to graduate with fragmented theoretical and practical knowledge means the learning curve right out of college is insanely higher and graduates don't actually know what to expect. Among my classmates its almost painfully obvious who has just taken classes without taking any extra initiative and who has put in extra time or effort to learn more. It reflects poorly on the value of my degree when people can graduate with practically no zero knowledge of what they can do or should be able to do.

While I'd agree that doing more than just attending classes is to be expected if you're pursuing any college degree, the amount of education that seems to be missing from my degree can't be ignored. Something needs to change.

# A Change to My Degree(s)?

I propose that "Computer Science" as a degree should be put together as a math-heavy degree which really just extends or modifies the traditional math degree with more classes geared to toward the practical applications of math in computing. In fact I took a great class in my final semester, **Math 601: Cryptography**, which taught us the math behind crypto systems and how they're actually applied to computing. Classes such as these and other math classes such as Abstract Algebra are much more connected to the domain of computer science and should be required parts of the degree.

"Software Engineering" on the other hand should be its own degree, very similar to the "Computer Science" degree that I actually got, but with more emphasis on collaboration and use of developer tools (yes including AI). Especially with the way software engineering is heading these days its more important than ever that graduates know how to build and develop with tools, read and write code effectively, and communicate well with others.

The clear separation of "Computer Science" from "Software Engineering" I feel will allow for a better education and graduates who are better equipped for either the job market and or graduate school.
